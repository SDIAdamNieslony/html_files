Introduction

The fatigue life probability distribution of a given element subjected to fatigue load can be obtained by repeatedly carrying out the fatigue test with the same load condition for a sample with the same geometry. The obtained fatigue lifes will be characterized by a certain dispersion around a given mean value, creating a life probability distribution
Fig. 1. Simple SN curve with lifetime probability density at one selected load level
Let us consider the case of uniaxial fatigue tests of samples constructed as any multiple of the base sample. The base sample may be as shown below on the left. By combining consecutive samples "one by one", we obtain subsequent samples that are a multiple of the base sample.
Fig. 1. Graphical interpretation of single, double and triple samples
It is obvious that the durability of samples with the shape of a multiple of the base sample will be determined by the durability of the weakest base sample, which can be treated as a single one. The question is, what will happen to the probability distribution of multiple samples compared to the base sample? Will it be the same or will it change?

Let us perform a simulation in which we determine the probability distributions of the fatigue life of multiple samples by selecting the lowest value from the fatigue life of basic samples from which multiple samples are constructed. This approach expresses the assumption that the fatigue life of samples composed of base samples is determined by the weakest element according to the theory of the weakest link. We will perform the simulation in such a way that it is possible to change the number of fatigue tests for one load level and the multiplicity of the remaining multiple samples.
% number of fatigue tests at one load level
% 100000 is the number of tests that make the distributions well enough
n = 10000;
 
% the maximum number of base samples from which multiple samples were made
k = 10;
 
% number of cycles to failure N for each basic sample
N = 10.^(5 + 0.5*randn(n,k));
 
% logarithm of the number of cycles
logN = log10(N);
It should be remembered that the number of "fatigue tests results" is very large, but they are virtual tests. Please see the figure below which shows first series of the log life.
figure
histogram(logN(:, 1), 'Normalization','pdf')
xlabel('Log_{10}(N)'), ylabel('p(\sigma_a)')
We calculate the durability of the multiple samples (see Fig. (2)) by looking for the lowest durability of the basic samples from which the multiple samples were constructed.
for n = 2:k
NMultipleSample = logN(:, 1:n)'; % extracting the lives
% life for samples composed of n base samples
logN(:, n) = min(NMultipleSample);
end
As we recall, the normal distribution generator was used to calculate the lifetime of individual samples. We make a "normal probability plot". The Normal Probability Plot is a graphical technique to identify significant deviations from normal PDF.
figure
normplot(logN)
xlabel('fatigue life, log_{10}(N)')
We can see in the figure that the fatigue life of the basic sample corresponds to the normal distribution. "Multiple" samples are characterized by an increasing deviation from the normal distribution. In addition, the expected value and the variance of the random variable fatigue life variable also change. The scale of the change can be visualized on PDF plot for base sample and the last multiple sample from the series.
figure
logNBinWidth = (max(logN(:))-min(logN(:)))/40;
histogram(logN(:,1),'Normalization','pdf','BinWidth',logNBinWidth)
hold on
histogram(logN(:,end),'Normalization','pdf','BinWidth',logNBinWidth)
xlimits = get(gca,'XLim');
xi=linspace(xlimits(1),xlimits(2),200);
yi1=ksdensity(logN(:,1),xi);
yi5=ksdensity(logN(:,end),xi);
plot(xi,[yi1; yi5],'LineWidth',2)
hold off
xlabel('fatigue life, log_{10}(N)')
ylabel('p(log_{10}(N))')
legend('base sample',['multiple sample made of ' num2str(k) ' base samples'])
The changes of the mean and standard deviation of the fatigue life for all samples (base sample and multiple samples) are shown below

To check whether the distributions of the log fatigue life are normal, a Anderson-Darling test was performed for selected multiple sample.
SampleMultiple = 2;
warning off
h = adtest(logN(:, SampleMultiple));
warning on
if h == 0
disp(' ! PDF are Gaussian')
else
disp(' ! PDF are other then Gaussian')
end
! PDF are other then Gaussian

[f1, x1] = ecdf(logN(:, 1)); % base sample
[f2, x2] = ecdf(logN(:, 2)); % multiple sample made from 2

[f8, x8] = ecdf(logN(:, 8)); % multiple sample made from 8
